NBA Player Performance Analysis: The Three-Point Revolution

Question

How has three-point shooting evolved in the NBA, and which positions have adapted most to the modern era of basketball?

This analysis examines NBA player statistics to understand shooting patterns across different positions and how the game has shifted toward perimeter scoring.

Intended Audience

This analysis is designed for basketball analysts and coaches interested in positional trends, NBA fans curious about the evolution of the game, and sports data scientists studying performance metrics.

Data Source

The data comes from Basketball Reference (Sports Reference LLC 2024), one of the most comprehensive sources for NBA statistics. The dataset includes player statistics from the 2023-24 NBA season and can be accessed at Basketball Reference - NBA Player Stats.

NoteData Dictionary

The following variables are included in this analysis:

Player - Player name
Pos - Position (PG=Point Guard, SG=Shooting Guard, SF=Small Forward, PF=Power Forward, C=Center)
Age - Player age in years
Tm - Team abbreviation
G - Games played
MP - Minutes played per game
PTS - Points per game
TPA - Three-point attempts per game
TP_pct - Three-point shooting percentage
FG_pct - Field goal percentage
TRB - Total rebounds per game

Data Preparation

To ensure data quality and focus on regular rotation players, I filtered the dataset to include only players who averaged at least 25 minutes per game and appeared in at least 50 games during the season. This removes players with limited sample sizes and focuses the analysis on established contributors.

I then created several calculated variables to better understand shooting patterns. The three-point rate metric normalizes attempts per 10 minutes of playing time, allowing for fair comparisons between starters and bench players. I also grouped positions into three categories: Guards (PG and SG), Forwards (SF and PF), and Centers (C). This simplification helps reveal broader positional trends without getting lost in the nuances of hybrid positions.

Code 
nba_filtered <- nba_data %>%
  filter(MP >= 25, G >= 50)

nba_enhanced <- nba_filtered %>%
  mutate(
    three_point_rate = TPA / (MP / 10),
    scoring_efficiency = PTS / FG_pct,
    position_group = case_when(
      Pos %in% c("PG", "SG") ~ "Guards",
      Pos %in% c("SF", "PF") ~ "Forwards",
      Pos == "C" ~ "Centers"
    )
  )

position_stats <- nba_enhanced %>%
  group_by(position_group) %>%
  summarize(
    avg_3PA = mean(TPA, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_3P_pct = mean(TP_pct, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_PTS = mean(PTS, na.rm = TRUE),
    player_count = n()
  )

top_shooters <- nba_enhanced %>%
  arrange(desc(TPA)) %>%
  select(Player, Pos, TPA, TP_pct, PTS)

shooting_metrics <- nba_enhanced %>%
  select(Player, position_group, TPA, TP_pct) %>%
  pivot_longer(
    cols = c(TPA, TP_pct),
    names_to = "metric",
    values_to = "value"
  )

Results

Positional Differences in Three-Point Shooting

The first analysis examines how different position groups approach three-point shooting. As shown in Figure 1, guards clearly lead the league in perimeter attempts, averaging 6.7 three-point shots per game. Forwards sit in the middle at 4.8 attempts, while centers lag far behind at just 1.1 attempts per game. This stark difference reflects the fundamental roles each position plays in modern NBA offenses, with guards serving as primary floor spacers and centers maintaining their traditional interior focus.

The Volume-Accuracy Trade-off

A key question in basketball analytics is whether high-volume shooting compromises accuracy. The scatter plot below explores this relationship, with each point representing a player and the bubble size indicating their scoring output. The trend line shows a slight negative correlation, but many high-volume shooters maintain strong percentages. Players like Stephen Curry and Damian Lillard demonstrate that it’s possible to take 9-11 three-pointers per game while shooting above 35%, challenging the traditional notion that volume necessarily hurts efficiency.

Position-Specific Scoring Patterns

The faceted visualization below breaks down the relationship between scoring and rebounding across the three position groups. Each panel tells a different story about how these positions contribute to team success. Guards show high scoring with low rebounding, consistent with their perimeter-oriented roles. Forwards demonstrate balanced contributions in both areas. Centers dominate the glass while scoring efficiently, though generally at lower volumes than perimeter players. The color gradient by age reveals that elite production isn’t limited to any particular age group, with both veterans and young stars excelling.

Summary

This analysis reveals the profound impact of the three-point revolution in modern NBA basketball. Guards have fully embraced perimeter shooting, averaging 6.7 three-point attempts per game compared to just 1.1 for centers. Despite the high volume, elite shooters maintain accuracy above 35%, demonstrating that the strategic shift toward three-point shooting is both efficient and effective. The data shows clear positional specialization, with guards serving as primary perimeter threats while centers focus on interior scoring and rebounding. These findings align with broader trends in basketball analytics (Oliver 2024), which emphasize the mathematical advantage of three-point shooting. The modern NBA has successfully integrated high-volume three-point shooting without sacrificing efficiency, fundamentally transforming the game’s strategic landscape.
